Password Safety Class

Introduction to Internet Safety & Passwords

The internet is an incredible tool. You can learn, create, communicate, and explore new ideas. But it also has dangers—hackers, scams, and identity theft can threaten your personal information. One of the most important ways to protect yourself online is with strong passwords.

Think of your password as a key to a treasure chest. If someone steals that key, they could access everything inside: your email, social media, gaming accounts, school work, or even banking apps in the future. That’s why understanding password safety is critical.

Why Passwords Are Important

  • Protection: Prevents hackers from accessing your accounts.
  • Privacy: Keeps personal messages, photos, and information safe.
  • Security: Stops identity theft and scams.

Risks of Weak Passwords

Hackers can guess weak passwords like 123456 or password in seconds using automated tools. Once they gain access, they can:

  • Send spam or malicious messages using your account.
  • Change your password so you lose access.
  • Access connected accounts, like email or social media, for further attacks.

Fun Fact: Millions of people still use “password” as their password! Hackers know this, making weak passwords extremely vulnerable.

Lesson Goals

  • Learn what makes a password strong.
  • Create passwords that are easy to remember but hard to guess.
  • Use 2FA and password managers to protect accounts.
  • Recognize phishing, public Wi-Fi dangers, and common mistakes.
Mini Exercise: List 3 online accounts you use frequently. Rate your current password for each as weak, medium, or strong.

Characteristics of Strong Passwords

A strong password is your first defense against hackers. Key characteristics:

1. Length

Passwords should be at least 12 characters long. Longer passwords are harder to guess.

2. Complexity

Include a mix of:

  • Uppercase letters (A-Z)
  • Lowercase letters (a-z)
  • Numbers (0-9)
  • Symbols (!, $, #, %, etc.)

3. Unpredictability

Avoid common words, sequences, or repeated characters.

Weak examples: 12345678, abcdef, password, qwerty

Strong examples: G7$kLp9#zR!2, Sun$et_42Wave!

Tips for Creating Strong Passwords

  • Use a random mix of letters, numbers, and symbols.
  • Avoid using personal info (like birthdays or pets’ names).
  • Make it unique for every account.
Mini Exercise: Create 2 strong passwords for two different accounts without writing personal info.

Passphrases – Easy to Remember, Hard to Guess

A passphrase is a combination of words that forms a sentence or phrase. It’s easier to remember than random letters and symbols but still very secure.

Examples: "BluePizzaRunsFast!2025", "MyDogEats3BigCookies!"

Tips for Creating Passphrases

  • Choose 3–4 random words.
  • Add numbers or symbols.
  • Make them personal but not obvious.

Why Passphrases Work

Hackers often use “dictionary attacks,” where they try common words. A passphrase with unique words and symbols is extremely difficult to guess.

Mini Exercise: Create a passphrase using your favorite color, animal, and number. Example: "GreenTiger42Rocks!"

Avoiding Personal Information in Passwords

Many people use their birthday, name, or pet’s name as a password. This is risky because hackers can find this information on social media.

Examples of Bad Passwords

Emily2008, Fluffy123

Better Approach

Make it abstract but memorable. Combine unrelated words with symbols and numbers.

Example: "Moon$7Tiger!Lake"

Tip: Even a favorite song lyric or movie quote can be turned into a strong password.

Mini Exercise: Look at your current passwords and identify which ones include personal info. Rewrite them in a safer, abstract format.

The Danger of Reusing Passwords

Using the same password for multiple accounts is risky. If one account gets hacked, all others using that password are in danger.

Real-Life Example

Hackers accessing your email could then compromise social media, gaming, and shopping accounts. This can lead to identity theft, money theft, or spam.

Safe Practice

Use unique passwords for each account. Password managers make this easier by remembering everything securely.

Mini Exercise: Make a list of accounts that share the same password. Plan to update them with unique, strong passwords this week.

How Hackers Steal Passwords

Hackers use many techniques to steal passwords. Understanding these methods can help you avoid being a victim.

1. Phishing

Phishing is when hackers send fake emails or messages to trick you into giving your password. Example: “Your account will be deleted. Click here to verify your password!”

2. Keylogging

Some malicious software records every key you press, including your password.

3. Data Breaches

Sometimes websites are hacked, and usernames and passwords are leaked online. If you reuse passwords, hackers can access multiple accounts.

4. Guessing

Hackers use common words, sequences, or leaked passwords to guess yours. Tip: Always be cautious of suspicious messages and avoid reusing passwords.

Exercise: Look at your email inbox. Can you identify any suspicious or phishing emails? Don’t click any links!

Recognizing Phishing Scams

Phishing is one of the most common ways hackers steal accounts.

Red Flags

  • Emails with poor grammar or spelling.
  • Urgent messages demanding you act immediately.
  • Links that don’t match the real website.

Example: “URGENT! Click this link to confirm your account or you’ll lose access!”

Safety Tips

  • Don’t click links from unknown sources.
  • Always go to the official website instead of clicking the email link.
  • Enable 2FA for extra protection.
Exercise: Write an example of a phishing message someone might send you, then explain how you would avoid it.

Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

2FA is an extra layer of security. Even if a hacker gets your password, they cannot access your account without the second factor.

Types of 2FA

  • SMS codes sent to your phone.
  • Authenticator apps like Google Authenticator.
  • Email verification codes.
Example: You log in with your password. The site sends a code to your phone. You must enter this code to complete login.

Tip: Always enable 2FA for email, social media, and gaming accounts.

Exercise: Check one of your accounts and enable 2FA if it’s available.

Using Password Managers

Password managers store all your passwords in one secure app. They can also generate strong passwords for you.

Popular Managers

  • LastPass
  • Bitwarden
  • 1Password

Benefits

  • You only need to remember the master password.
  • You can generate long, complex passwords for every account.
  • Your passwords are encrypted and safe.
Exercise: Research one password manager and note how it stores your passwords safely.

Creating a Password System

A password system is a way to make passwords unique but memorable.

Example System

Take the first two letters of the website, add your favorite number, add a special character.

Example:

  • Gmail → Gi42$!
  • Facebook → Fa42$!
Exercise: Create a password for three of your accounts using this system.

Updating Passwords Regularly

Passwords should not stay the same forever. Updating them regularly reduces the risk of being hacked.

Tips

  • Change passwords every 3–6 months.
  • Update immediately if a website announces a breach.
  • Keep a list of accounts you’ve updated to track progress.
Exercise: List your top 5 accounts and plan a date to update each password.

Safe Password Storage

Never write passwords on sticky notes left on your desk. Avoid saving them in unprotected files on your devices.

Safe Storage Methods

  • Password managers (encrypted storage)
  • Secure offline notes (locked devices)

Tip: If you must write passwords down temporarily, destroy the paper once the password is safely stored.

Exercise: Check where you currently store passwords and move them to a secure location if needed.

Public Wi-Fi Risks

Public Wi-Fi is convenient, but not always safe. Hackers can intercept information over public networks.

Safety Tips

  • Avoid logging into accounts on public Wi-Fi.
  • Use a VPN (Virtual Private Network) for extra encryption.
  • Log out immediately after using public Wi-Fi.
Exercise: Think about the last time you used public Wi-Fi. Did you log into any accounts? How could you have been safer?

Recognizing Compromised Accounts

Sometimes accounts get hacked without your knowledge.

Signs Your Account May Be Compromised

  • Login alerts from unknown devices.
  • Emails sent that you didn’t write.
  • Passwords changed without your permission.

What to Do

  • Change your password immediately.
  • Enable 2FA.
  • Review your account settings for suspicious activity.
  • Notify friends if your account was sending spam.
Exercise: Check your recent account activity on one social media account.

Social Media Safety

Social media is fun, but hackers often target it. Protect your accounts by being mindful of the information you share.

What to Avoid Sharing

  • Birthdays, full name, hometown
  • Pet names
  • School or class schedules

Password Tip: Never use personal info in passwords. Hackers can find it online and guess your password easily.

Exercise: Audit your social media accounts and list any personal information that could make a password guessable. Then, plan a new password that avoids this info.
