Password Safety Class

Introduction to Internet Safety & Passwords

The internet is an incredible tool. You can learn, create, communicate, and explore new ideas. But it also has dangers—hackers, scams, and identity theft can threaten your personal information. One of the most important ways to protect yourself online is with strong passwords.

Think of your password as a key to a treasure chest. If someone steals that key, they could access everything inside: your email, social media, gaming accounts, school work, or even banking apps in the future. That’s why understanding password safety is critical.

Why Passwords Are Important

  • Protection: Prevents hackers from accessing your accounts.
  • Privacy: Keeps personal messages, photos, and information safe.
  • Security: Stops identity theft and scams.

Risks of Weak Passwords

Hackers can guess weak passwords like 123456 or password in seconds using automated tools. Once they gain access, they can:

  • Send spam or malicious messages using your account.
  • Change your password so you lose access.
  • Access connected accounts, like email or social media, for further attacks.

Fun Fact: Millions of people still use “password” as their password! Hackers know this, making weak passwords extremely vulnerable.

Lesson Goals

  • Learn what makes a password strong.
  • Create passwords that are easy to remember but hard to guess.
  • Use 2FA and password managers to protect accounts.
  • Recognize phishing, public Wi-Fi dangers, and common mistakes.
Mini Exercise: List 3 online accounts you use frequently. Rate your current password for each as weak, medium, or strong.

Characteristics of Strong Passwords

A strong password is your first defense against hackers. Key characteristics:

1. Length

Passwords should be at least 12 characters long. Longer passwords are harder to guess.

2. Complexity

Include a mix of:

  • Uppercase letters (A-Z)
  • Lowercase letters (a-z)
  • Numbers (0-9)
  • Symbols (!, $, #, %, etc.)

3. Unpredictability

Avoid common words, sequences, or repeated characters.

Weak examples: 12345678, abcdef, password, qwerty

Strong examples: G7$kLp9#zR!2, Sun$et_42Wave!

Tips for Creating Strong Passwords

  • Use a random mix of letters, numbers, and symbols.
  • Avoid using personal info (like birthdays or pets’ names).
  • Make it unique for every account.
Mini Exercise: Create 2 strong passwords for two different accounts without writing personal info.

Passphrases – Easy to Remember, Hard to Guess

A passphrase is a combination of words that forms a sentence or phrase. It’s easier to remember than random letters and symbols but still very secure.

Examples: "BluePizzaRunsFast!2025", "MyDogEats3BigCookies!"

Tips for Creating Passphrases

  • Choose 3–4 random words.
  • Add numbers or symbols.
  • Make them personal but not obvious.

Why Passphrases Work

Hackers often use “dictionary attacks,” where they try common words. A passphrase with unique words and symbols is extremely difficult to guess.

Mini Exercise: Create a passphrase using your favorite color, animal, and number. Example: "GreenTiger42Rocks!"

Avoiding Personal Information in Passwords

Many people use their birthday, name, or pet’s name as a password. This is risky because hackers can find this information on social media.

Examples of Bad Passwords

Emily2008, Fluffy123

Better Approach

Make it abstract but memorable. Combine unrelated words with symbols and numbers.

Example: "Moon$7Tiger!Lake"

Tip: Even a favorite song lyric or movie quote can be turned into a strong password.

Mini Exercise: Look at your current passwords and identify which ones include personal info. Rewrite them in a safer, abstract format.

The Danger of Reusing Passwords

Using the same password for multiple accounts is risky. If one account gets hacked, all others using that password are in danger.

Real-Life Example

Hackers accessing your email could then compromise social media, gaming, and shopping accounts. This can lead to identity theft, money theft, or spam.

Safe Practice

Use unique passwords for each account. Password managers make this easier by remembering everything securely.

Mini Exercise: Make a list of accounts that share the same password. Plan to update them with unique, strong passwords this week.
